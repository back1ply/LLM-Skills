# Meetings & Improvement Cycle

## PDCA Improvement Cycle

KPIs sit in the **Check** step. The cycle's effectiveness is limited by its weakest element.

### Step 1 — Plan

- [ ] Clear strategy or set of strategic objectives to work towards
- [ ] Can clearly explain what success looks like
- [ ] Stakeholders identified and documented (ideally using RACI matrix)
- [ ] Stakeholders are aware they are stakeholders
- [ ] Day-to-day level: clear on what you're trying to achieve
- [ ] Timescale associated with objectives
- [ ] Clear ownership assigned
- [ ] Correct resources allocated
- [ ] Documented what you're trying to achieve
- [ ] Baseline KPI measures have: measure definition, frequency, owner

### Step 2 — Do

- [ ] Execution plan in place
- [ ] Project plan reviewed regularly
- [ ] Required resources available and budgeted
- [ ] Appropriate tools available
- [ ] Planned stakeholder time made available as agreed
- [ ] Delivery teams meeting at required frequency
- [ ] Actions recorded and managed through action log

### Step 3 — Check

- [ ] At least 10-30 representative data points recorded
- [ ] Data summarised in a meaningful way
- [ ] Data presented with required features but no visual clutter
- [ ] Data tables present where required
- [ ] Appropriate, relevant, and rational commentary added
- [ ] Analysis shared with key stakeholders from Plan step
- [ ] Output shared in good time for preparation and judgement
- [ ] Mechanisms by which real-world events drive the KPI are understood

If the last answer is "no", consider structured problem-solving (e.g., P-M Analysis).

### Step 4 — Act

- [ ] All stakeholders agree on importance of action
- [ ] Dissent and disagreement discussed and resolved
- [ ] Adequate resources in place
- [ ] Necessary process downtime agreed with process owners
- [ ] Resources available for fixing issues
- [ ] Budget available (if required)
- [ ] Notes on changes and fixes made and shared for future reference

### Making the Cycle Work

Multiple passes are always needed before things work properly. Awareness of how the steps join together develops a process "conscience". Apply PDCA to PDCA itself.

## Meeting Effectiveness

### Signs of Poor Data-Free Meeting Behaviour

- Frequent shouting
- Most senior opinion wins
- Over-analysis without evidence or data
- Failure to agree meaningful, practical actions
- Lack of documented actions
- Regular serious over-runs
- Senior stakeholders deliberately dodging attendance

### Chairing Meetings Checklist

- [ ] Challenge attendees who are not prepared and haven't looked at the data
- [ ] Make sure data is used at relevant points
- [ ] Intercept fruitless arguments with an action to gather information for next session
- [ ] Keep the meeting to schedule
- [ ] Use techniques like the lightning round to stop ramblers

**On rotating chairs**: Fine in theory, but can undermine behaviour change efforts. Consider a static Chair for at least a few meetings until behaviours improve.

### The Lightning Round

For contributors prone to rambling. Give a fixed interval (typically 60 seconds) for each update. Key phrases: "prior thought" and "preparation". The lightning round promotes these behaviours and highlights when people are "winging it".

## Meeting Relevancy

### Diagnosing the Problem

If meetings are long and tedious, the root cause may be poorly defined scope with topics irrelevant to most attendees.

### Relevancy Matrix

Create a matrix with attendees on one axis and discussion topics on the other. Mark relevance for each intersection:

| Topic | CEO | Head of Risk | Head of IT | Head of Finance | Head of Sales |
|-------|-----|-------------|-----------|----------------|--------------|
| Financial performance | Relevant | Some | Not relevant | Relevant | Relevant |
| Complaints | Some | Relevant | Not relevant | Not relevant | Relevant |
| Health and safety | Relevant | Relevant | Some | Not relevant | Some |

### Fixing Relevancy Problems

1. **Section the meeting** — Bring certain people in for certain relevant sections only
2. **Spin off** — Split elements into separate meetings

## Meeting Rules

Example rules (formulate and agree with participants):

- [ ] Agenda and data issued X days (hours) before the meeting
- [ ] Expectation of "arrive on time or don't come"
- [ ] Listen to every contribution
- [ ] One person speaks at a time
- [ ] Phones and computers off
- [ ] Take problem solving outside the meeting
- [ ] Actions and minutes issued within two hours of meeting end
- [ ] Review previous actions

**Reality**: Meeting behaviour is determined by the competence and behaviour of the Chair, not the written rules.

## Report Clarity in Meetings

### Are Reports Understandable?

- [ ] Does the report make sense to you?
- [ ] Have you asked internal customers whether it makes sense to them?
- [ ] Has it been tested on an intelligent but non-specialist layperson?
- [ ] Have you specifically asked how clarity could be improved?
- [ ] Does the report require specialist briefing? If so, how do people access it?

### Keeping Stakeholders On-Side

No one likes being cornered, especially in front of their boss. Share information in a way that gives everyone the opportunity to prepare properly, rather than being broadsided by unexpected results.

## Managing Ad Hoc Requests

When ad hoc reporting requests impair business-as-usual:

- [ ] How many ad hoc requests are you handling?
- [ ] How much resource do they take?
- [ ] What scheduled work is suffering?
- [ ] Do ad hoc requests show weaknesses in the standard report schedule?
- [ ] Should ad hoc work become a regular output?
- [ ] What scope for self-service tools?

---

## Capturing Meeting Actions

- [ ] Agreed person/method for recording actions?
- [ ] Action description clearly captured?
- [ ] Reason for the action captured?
- [ ] Action assigned to a specific person?
- [ ] Timescale assigned?
- [ ] Previous actions formally reviewed and updated?

If your meeting does not generate well-defined actions, question whether it is adding any value.
