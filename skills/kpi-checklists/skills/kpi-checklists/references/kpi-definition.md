# KPI Definition

## The "Common Sense" Problem

Many KPIs use descriptive names that create dangerous assumptions. Example: "Utilisation" (ratio of billed hours to available hours) seems simple but raises questions:

- Available hours: nominal or actual? Include overtime?
- Holidays or sickness: zero utilisation?
- Individual: only billable staff, or support staff too?
- Mandatory commitments (safety training): in available hours?
- Sales activity: value-add but not billable — how to count?
- Reduced-rate billing: counts the same as full-rate?
- Dual-role staff: only billable portion?

The solution: a KPI Definition Database. Could be a Word document or Excel spreadsheet — the format matters less than the content.

---

## KPI Definition Checklist

Answer these for every KPI:

- [ ] **KPI name** — Use a "what-it-says-is-what-it-is" name. Be careful with "efficiency" and "effectiveness" — everyone has a strong view that their usage is correct.

- [ ] **Measurement intent** — Describes the measure and reasoning behind its selection. "Why are you measuring this?"

- [ ] **KPI definition/formula** — Detailed formula for calculating the numeric value. Test: "Could a reasonably numerate stranger calculate the value using this definition and relevant source data?"

- [ ] **Frequency of update** — How often it's calculated. Consider "end effects" where the reporting cycle may create overlap errors. Long cycles lessen this; short cycles make it more acute.

- [ ] **Units of measure** — Dimensionless ratio (e.g., efficiency) or a real measure with dimensions (kilograms, money, calls per day)?

- [ ] **Notes/Assumptions** — Clarify terms and highlight key assumptions. Almost all KPIs have flaws — document them openly to avoid flawed analyses.

- [ ] **KPI information availability** — Readily available, available with some effort, or not available. Gives a hit list for automating and streamlining.

- [ ] **Data elements and source** — Painful level of detail: which server, which directory, which spreadsheet cell. Include naming conventions where documents cover a certain period.

- [ ] **Target (where known)** — What is the target value?

- [ ] **Source for, and approach to, setting targets** — Where does the target come from? Why that level? (It's embarrassing not to know this.)

- [ ] **Person responsible for target setting** — One person must ultimately be responsible, even if agreed by consensus.

- [ ] **Person accountable for set targets** — Carries the "strategic can". Should be consulted but may not set the actual value.

- [ ] **Person responsible for tracking and reporting** — Who manages the day-to-day process?

### Benefits of KPI Definitions

- Forces the organisation to clarify and discuss definitions
- Weaknesses and uncertainties are written down and "out in the open"
- Avoids similar-sounding KPIs calculated in different ways
- Reference document for anyone unclear on how a measure is calculated

**Critical rule**: Definitions must be held in a single managed location to prevent multiple conflicting versions.

## Meeting Terms of Reference

### Why Create a Terms of Reference?

Most meetings should centre on decisions based on good information. Without being clear on what a meeting is for, you can't supply the right reports and dashboards.

### Terms of Reference Checklist

- [ ] What is the **purpose** of the meeting?
- [ ] What is the **scope**? (Which part of the organisation, approval level, etc.)
- [ ] What are the **inputs**? (Data, reports, samples, etc.)
- [ ] When should attendees get **reports/documents** prior to the meeting? (SLA for production)
- [ ] What are the **outputs**? (Decisions, budget approval, judgements, sign-off, etc.)
- [ ] **Who should be there?** (By role and authority)
- [ ] Is there clear guidance on **deputies** and their authority?
- [ ] **How often** does the meeting happen?
- [ ] **Who runs** the meeting? (By role. Include contingency owners.)
- [ ] What are the **roles and responsibilities**? (Minute taking, organisation, timetable, etc.)
- [ ] **Where** does the meeting happen?
- [ ] **How long** is the meeting?
- [ ] How do you **change the Terms of Reference**? Who to contact?

### Going Live with Terms of Reference

The most effective approach is gently but persistently. "Big bang" introductions don't last. Requires sustained reinforcement. The Chair is in the best position to exert this sustained pressure — so choose them wisely.
