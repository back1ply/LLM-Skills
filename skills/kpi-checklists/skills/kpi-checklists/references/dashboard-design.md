# Dashboard Design

## Prototyping Approaches

### Why Prototype?

It's rare to find a client who knows precisely what they want. Prototyping avoids massive wasted effort — even with a good process, expect 5-10 design iterations. Building fully working reports over every iteration wastes developer/analyst resource.

### Three Prototyping Options

| Option | Description | Best For |
|--------|-------------|----------|
| **1. Offline** | Create versions off-line, present to decision-makers | Lowest risk, but slowest. Good for indecisive clients. |
| **2. Live** | Build dashboard in a live session with decision-makers | Patient, engaged decision-makers. Need tool proficiency. |
| **3. Hybrid** | Present pre-prepared designs, tune together in session (Recommended) | Best balance of efficiency and rapid prototyping. |

### Prototyping Stages

**Stage 1** — Discuss and agree principles of good design. Stick to evidence. Extreme restraint in data presentation leads to better understanding.

**Stage 2** — Watch how current reports are really used:
- Ask people how they currently use reports
- Sit in on meetings and observe actual use
- Look for differences between what you're told and what you observe
- Big differences suggest a "reality gap" — possibly a behavioural problem, not a reporting one

**Stage 3** — Create design options. Decision-makers respond well to mocked-up options. Presenting a range triggers valuable discussions.

**Stage 4** (optional) — Review and score existing reports using the design checklist below. Takes emotion out of the discussion.

### Getting Design Sign-Off

- Identify stakeholders using RACI matrix
- Agree a sign-off schedule to avoid endless revision
- Ensure there is a final arbiter (no "tug of war" between stakeholders)
- Get approvers to put approval in writing

## The Brilliant Dashboards Checklist

Score each question: 1 = poor, 5 = good

### Visual Design

- [ ] Is colour used to convey additional information?
- [ ] Are colours used consistently for the same meaning?
- [ ] Is a consistent design theme used for all charts and text boxes?
- [ ] Are there unnecessary boxes and dividers?
- [ ] Do dividers and boxes lead the eye in a helpful way?
- [ ] With RAG (traffic light) indicators, is it clear what criteria are used?
- [ ] Are there unnecessary tick marks?
- [ ] Are there unnecessary borders on chart areas?
- [ ] Is the background unnecessarily shaded?
- [ ] Are the columns/bars unnecessarily shaded?
- [ ] Are there unnecessary borders on columns?

### Layout

- [ ] Are text and chart size proportionate to the importance of the information?
- [ ] Are data points that need comparison near to each other?
- [ ] Are logos and ornamentation kept to a bare minimum?
- [ ] Is spacing consistent and pleasing?
- [ ] Are lines used to guide the eye in a meaningful direction?
- [ ] Is there a "logical hierarchy" for text and comments?

### Structure

- [ ] Are there large amounts of numbers that must be read to understand the high-level situation?
- [ ] Can you quickly navigate to the section you need?
- [ ] Is it clear where the biggest issues are and how to find more information?
- [ ] Are targets clearly different from data sets?

### Charts

- [ ] Do the graphs and charts meet your objectives?
- [ ] Are the charts intuitive (no need for careful study or explanation)?
- [ ] Do the charts have impact and give insight?
- [ ] Do charts allow meaningful comparison of relevant data sets?
- [ ] Do charts clearly show patterns and trends?
- [ ] Does understanding the document tax your short-term memory too much?
- [ ] Do your eyes have to leap about the page to understand?
- [ ] Do the charts answer an obvious question?
- [ ] If the chart uses 3D, is 3D actually required?
- [ ] Is the message clear?

### Axes

- [ ] Are the axes "fair" and labelled? (Watch for unlabelled logarithmic axes or axes not starting from zero.)
- [ ] Are fonts clear, the right size, and readable?
- [ ] If you use a double axis, is it required to make a valid point?

### Labelling

- [ ] Are all charts clearly labelled, avoiding jargon or acronyms?
- [ ] Is it clear what period the charts refer to?
- [ ] Are labels physically near the things they describe?
- [ ] If unavoidable, is jargon defined?
- [ ] Is the level of labelling appropriate (not obscuring the chart)?
- [ ] Are numbers given to realistic precision (not 5 decimal places when source data doesn't justify it)?

### Trending

- [ ] Is there meaningful trending?
- [ ] Are gridlines aiding or obscuring clarity?
- [ ] Is there unhelpful use of colour and area/fill patterns?

### Text

- [ ] Is the text relevant?
- [ ] Is the text concise?
- [ ] Is the text spelled correctly and without grammatical errors?
- [ ] Is it clear with which graph any text is associated?
- [ ] Is any additional text clear, the right size, and readable?

### Visual Clarity

- [ ] Is the document (and its sub-elements) an optimal size?
- [ ] Does the layout work for the delivery medium? (Smartphone, iPad, projector?)
- [ ] Do any of the intended audience have eyesight issues? Is the output suitable in size, colour, and contrast?

### Other Appearance and Readability

- [ ] Is it possible to understand the general message "at a glance"?
- [ ] Is it clear who created the report and contents?
- [ ] Is it clear who to contact for queries or corrections, and how?

## Report Readability Rules

- No font size below 8 point. Body text minimum 10 point.
- No red-to-green differentiation only (red-green colour blindness).
- No strongly-coloured text on strongly-coloured background.
- No overlapping data sets on charts.
- No double Y axis charts.
- No mixed chart types (e.g., bar with line).

## Report Length & Complexity

- [ ] How long would it take to read the complete document properly?
- [ ] How much data is there "just in case"?
- [ ] What justification exists for the just-in-case data?
- [ ] Are jargon terms and acronyms defined?
- [ ] Could an intelligent but non-specialist reader make sense of the report?

If there's a lot of "just-in-case" data, the report may service a poorly focused meeting. Review the meeting's Terms of Reference.

## Observing Real Usage

Watch how people use reports in meetings (without letting them know what you're observing):

- [ ] How often do they look at the documents?
- [ ] Do they appear to have read them in advance?
- [ ] Which bits do they look at? Refer to? Annotate?
- [ ] Are there sections that provoke debate?
- [ ] Are there parts ignored by everyone?
- [ ] Any comments about data quality or presentation to take on board?
